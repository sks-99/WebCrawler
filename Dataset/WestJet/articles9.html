WestJet's restart at Charlottetown Airport will have economic impact on P.E.I.

Terrence McEachern · Posted: July 17, 2021, 2:50 p.m. | Updated: a day ago | 5 Min Read

CHARLOTTETOWN, P.E.I. — With flight offerings expanding in Charlottetown, Doug Newson said the airport is starting to look like an airport again.

"We've got airlines adding flights as of this weekend. We've got three new flights starting later this month," said Newson, CEO of the Charlottetown Airport Authority on July 16. "It will mean that it will start to feel like an airport again. It already has. And it's only going to get better here as the next few weeks come. We won't be back to 2019 this year, but it certainly won't (resemble) 2020 either."

On July 15, Newson was part of a virtual roundtable restart discussion with WestJet executives, along with other provincial tourism, government and business leaders.

The roundtable discussion was led by WestJet executives Angela Avery, executive vice-president and Andy Gibbons, vice-president of government relations. Other P.E.I. participants included Corryn Clemence, CEO of the Tourism Industry Association of P.E.I. (TIAPEI), Charlottetown Mayor Philip Brown, Charlottetown MP Sean Casey and Nicole Bellefleur, interim CEO of the Greater Charlottetown Area Chamber of Commerce.

Beginning July 29, WestJet plans to offer a seasonal direct flight to Calgary twice a week out of the Charlottetown airport. That flight was first announced in early 2020, with plans to start in June of that year, but the pandemic hit and impacted air travel.

"My understanding is bookings are going well," said Newson. "We're obviously hoping if it does well this year and we're back to a bit of a normal tourism season in 2022, that flight will continue to grow in terms of the length of the season but also the number of days a week that they operate."

Recovery and restart

According to WestJet, the pandemic has resulted in a $995 million decline in travel and tourism revenue in Atlantic Canada and a $15 billion drop in revenue in Canada as a whole. In November 2020, due to low demand for air service, WestJet suspended flights to Moncton, Fredericton, Sydney and Charlottetown, and significantly reduced service to Halifax and St. John's. The company also cut staff by about 70 per cent.

Part of WestJet's restart plans involve people wanting to travel again. According to Avery, the company is seeing bookings come back again, including 19 per cent of Canadians intending to fly to Europe in the next six months, 28 per cent to the Caribbean, 33 per cent to the U.S. and 61 per cent domestically, including 19 per cent to Atlantic Canada.

Another part of the airline's restart plans is the ongoing advocacy concerning travel restrictions, which includes the federal government having a clear plan.

"I think the No. 1 priority for us is ensuring that fully vaccinated travellers are not segmented based on where they come from. So, whether you're American, British, French, or otherwise – fully vaccinated – we believe, in terms of the federal government's travel policies, should be treated as fully vaccinated. Because we do believe that is the science - and health-based policy that is required," said Gibbons.

Economic impact

Newson said that P.E.I.'s re-opening to the rest of Canada on July 18 is a "game-changer" for the airport in terms of its recovery. From April to September 2020, the Charlottetown airport saw air travel drop an average of 95 per cent compared to 2019. WestJet started offering a flight out of Charlottetown to Toronto again in June. Air Canada also currently has a Toronto and Montreal flight out of Charlottetown, and in late July and early August, two other airlines – Flair and PAL – are planning to offer flights out of the Charlottetown airport.

Avery said WestJet has been flying to and from P.E.I. since 2005. In pre-pandemic 2019, the company's economic impact on P.E.I. was $32 million. The company's operation on the Island has led directly to 46 jobs and another 200 jobs created indirectly.

Newson said the economic impact and job creation that WestJet has on P.E.I. was one highlight of the virtual roundtable, especially how the company referred to its role as an investment.

"You sometimes forget that you have an airline partner here, and we know it's important for the economy of P.E.I. We certainly missed them when they were gone. Now they've started re-hiring people. And having that air access through various partners, there's so many economic spinoffs and social benefits to having those flights," he said. "I like how they do call it an investment because we are competing with cities right across this country for them to invest their assets in our airport and our province."

Terrence McEachern is The Guardian's business reporter
